STORY 1: The Recovering Partner
A good partner has one bad month. How does the system respond -- and how do they recover?
The Setup
Imagine a partner in Agra running 45 active connections. For the last three evaluation cycles (each cycle is 7 days),
they've been COMPLIANT on all three SLA checks: uptime at 94% (threshold is 90%), service resolution at 6 hours
(threshold is 8 hours), and installation quality at 88% (threshold is 85%). They're in the ELIGIBLE_FULL exposure band -
meaning the system routes new customers to them at full priority. Their compensation bonus tier is GOOD. Everything is
working.
>> Quality OS evaluates every 7 days across 3 SLA domains. All must pass for COMPLIANT.
The Problem
In week 4, the partner hires a new technician who isn't properly trained. Installation quality drops sharply -- the next
evaluation scores IQ at 72%, well below the 85% threshold. The other two domains are still fine: uptime is 93%,
resolution is 5 hours. But one domain failing is enough.
The Quality OS moves the partner to NON_COMPLIANT on the Installation Integrity domain. This single state change
triggers a cascade across the system.
>> Quality OS -> NON_COMPLIANT triggers D&A; to set INELIGIBLE_QUALITY. Zero new customers.
The Cascade
The Demand & Allocation OS immediately moves the partner's exposure band to INELIGIBLE_QUALITY. No new
customers will be routed to them -- existing connections are unaffected, but growth stops completely. The Enforcement
OS receives the NC signal too: this is the first non-compliant window, so the performance track moves to
PATTERN_WATCH (E01 trigger). This isn't restriction yet -- it's the system saying "I'm watching." The Compensation OS
adjusts: with enforcement in PATTERN_WATCH and quality NON_COMPLIANT, the bonus tier drops to NONE. The
partner still earns the base Rs300 per recharge, but no bonus.
>> Enforcement OS: 1 NC = PATTERN_WATCH. It takes 3 more NC windows to reach RESTRICTED.
What the Partner Sees
On their dashboard, the partner sees their quality score has dropped and their status shows a decline. They see fewer
new customers being assigned. They see their bonus is gone this period. But they don't see the enforcement threshold
numbers, the exact routing formula, or how many more failures would trigger restriction. The Visibility OS deliberately
keeps this hidden -- the partner should focus on improving quality, not gaming exact thresholds.
>> Visibility OS: R1 (partner) sees outcomes, not methodology. Thresholds are SUPPRESSED.
The Recovery
The partner retrains their technician. Next week's evaluation: IQ is back to 89%. Quality OS moves them back to
COMPLIANT. But recovery isn't instant. The D&A; OS moves them to ELIGIBLE_REDUCED -- they get 50% of normal
routing for the next 2 cycles (the recovery window, P43). This is deliberate: the system needs to see sustained
improvement, not a one-week spike. After 3 consecutive COMPLIANT cycles (P42), they return to ELIGIBLE_FULL.
Enforcement clears PATTERN_WATCH once the quality pattern resolves. Bonus tier restores.
>> D&A; OS: Recovery = 2 cycles at 50% routing, then 3 COMPLIANT cycles for full restoration.
Key Takeaway
The system doesn't punish one bad week -- it watches for patterns. A partner who fixes the problem recovers fully, but
recovery takes time (2-3 evaluation cycles). The system rewards consistency over perfection. If the partner hadn't
fixed it and hit 3 more NC windows, enforcement would have escalated to RESTRICTED -- meaning not just zero new
customers, but active review of their entire operation.-- | -- | -
STORY 2: The Bad Actor
A partner is caught disintermediating -- directing Wiom customers to a competitor. What happens?
The Setup
A partner in Meerut has 48 active connections and looks fine on paper. Uptime is 91%, resolution is within limits,
installation quality is 86%. Quality OS has them as COMPLIANT. D&A; is routing new customers to them. Compensation
tier is GOOD. Nothing in the data suggests a problem.
The Discovery
A field visit reveals the truth: the partner has been telling customers to switch to a competitor's service. They're using
Wiom's installation infrastructure but redirecting the customer relationship. This is a First Principle Violation -- it's not poor
performance, it's adversarial behavior. An FPV investigation is opened. The Enforcement OS activates its Integrity track
(separate from the Performance track, which continues running independently). Investigation window: 7 days (E04).
>> Enforcement OS has two parallel tracks. Performance = patience + patterns. Integrity = verified violation = done.
FPV Confirmed -- The System Responds
Investigation confirms the disintermediation. This changes everything -- and it happens fast. The Enforcement OS
immediately moves posture to SUSPENDED. No warning. No watch period. No second chance. The D&A; OS receives
INELIGIBLE_ENFORCEMENT -- zero routing. Not reduced, zero. The Exit OS triggers a B2 Integrity Termination. Unlike
a voluntary exit or a B1 performance exit, B2 skips the 14-day notice period entirely. The partner goes directly from S1
(declared) to S3 (restricted). Risk overlay is set to R2 (Hostile) -- meaning all grace periods are bypassed.
>> Exit OS: B2 = no notice, no grace. Risk R2 = immediate restriction + reallocation.
The Reallocation
With R2 status, the normal 15% batch limit for reallocation is bypassed. All 48 connections are queued for immediate
rerouting. The D&A; OS runs its 4-stage ranking for each connection: which eligible partner in the zone has the best
quality, then the lowest load? Other Meerut partners start receiving these customers. The Connection Lifecycle OS
updates each connection: the customer's state stays ACTIVE -- they don't notice anything. Only the csp_id changes. The
customer keeps their WiFi; only the serving partner changes.
>> Connection Lifecycle: state persists through reassignment. CL_CSP_CHANGED emitted per connection.
Settlement & Offboarding
Once all connections are rerouted, the partner enters S5 (Offboarding). The Asset Custody OS marks all NetBox devices
as RETRIEVAL_PENDING. The partner must return them. The Payment OS processes in two tranches: Tranche 1
settles any earned compensation up to the exit date. Tranche 2 -- the deposit refund -- only happens after ALL devices
reach a terminal state (RETURNED, DAMAGED, or LOST). If devices are LOST, full recovery is deducted from the
deposit. S5 cannot close until both conditions are met: custody confirmed AND settlement complete. No timeout forces
this.
>> Exit OS S5 gate: BOTH custody confirmation + settlement complete required. No force-close.
Key Takeaway
The system treats integrity violations fundamentally differently from performance problems. Performance = the system
is patient, looks for patterns, gives time to recover. Integrity = one verified violation ends the relationship. There's no
recovery path from a confirmed FPV. The two tracks run independently -- a partner can be in PATTERN_WATCH on
performance while simultaneously being investigated on integrity. If integrity confirms, it overrides everything.

STORY 3: The Zone Crisis
One partner dominates a zone. Another exits. How does the system prevent a monopoly and keep
customers served?
The Setup
Meerut is classified as ZC-A (urban). Three partners operate here. Partner A has 35 active connections (58% of zone).
Partner B has 18 (30%). Partner C has 7 (12%). Total: 60 connections. Each partner has a cap of 50 (P46). The
concentration alert threshold is 60% (P56) -- Partner A is close at 58%, but hasn't triggered it yet. The zone is technically
REDUNDANT: multiple eligible partners, no single one over 60%.
>> Capacity OS tracks zone redundancy. REDUNDANT = healthy. CONCENTRATED = one partner >60%.
Partner B Exits
Partner B decides to leave -- voluntary exit. Exit OS processes: S0 -> S1 (declared) -> S2 (notice period, 14 days).
During notice, B continues serving their 18 connections normally. But the system is already planning. Capacity OS
calculates COVERAGE_STATE_ALTERNATE_AVAILABLE: can the remaining partners absorb B's load? Partner A has
headroom of 15 (cap 50 minus 35 active). Partner C has headroom of 43 (cap 50 minus 7). Combined headroom: 58. B's
connections: 18. 58 is well above 30% of 18 (which is ~5). Coverage is available.
>> Exit OS checks Capacity OS: is there enough alternate coverage? This gates S4 execution.
Reallocation & The Concentration Spike
Notice expires. B moves to S3 -> S4 (execution). D&A; begins rerouting B's 18 connections. The routing engine applies
its 4-stage process: eligibility check, quality rank, utilization rank, tiebreaker. Both A and C are COMPLIANT -- same
quality tier. So utilization rank decides: C has 12% load ratio (7/50), A has 70% (35/50). C gets priority. Most of B's
customers go to C. But let's say A picks up 7 and C picks up 11. After reallocation: A has 42 connections (65% of zone),
C has 18 (35%). Partner A just crossed the 60% concentration threshold.
>> D&A; OS routes by quality then load -- not by relationship or request. System-led, always.
The Escalation
Capacity OS detects: Partner A is now at 65%, above the 60% alert (P56). Zone state flips from REDUNDANT to
CONCENTRATED. This starts the 3-tier escalation clock from the Zone Replaceability Floor (INV-DAO-16). Tier 1 (after
3 cycles for ZC-A): ZONE_REDUNDANCY_PRESSURE -- advisory signal. The system notices but doesn't act yet. Tier 2
(after 6 cycles): ZONE_REDUNDANCY_CRITICAL -- binding. Capacity OS now requires a remediation plan within 2
cycles. The plan must include: what action, what timeline, what outcome. Tier 3 (after 9 cycles): if unresolved, D&A;
auto-applies ELIGIBLE_CAPPED to Partner A. No new connections routed to A in this zone. Existing 42 are untouched,
but growth stops.
>> Capacity OS: 3-tier escalation. Advisory -> Binding -> Auto-cap. ZC-A escalates fastest (1x multiplier).
Resolution
The system's response to concentration is to grow alternatives, not punish the dominant partner. A new Partner D is
onboarded into the zone. They start at ELIGIBLE_BASELINE (30% exposure, P53) for their first 2 ramp cycles (P45). As
D takes on connections and A's share naturally decreases below 60%, the zone returns to REDUNDANT. A's
ELIGIBLE_CAPPED is lifted. The zone is now healthier: three partners instead of two, better distributed.
Key Takeaway
No partner owns a territory. The system actively prevents concentration through escalating pressure -- first a warning,
then a mandate, then automated enforcement. When a partner exits, customers don't suffer: the system reroutes
based on quality and capacity, not relationships. The zone self-heals. This is what 'no territory entitlement' looks like in
practice.-- | -- | -
